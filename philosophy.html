Philosophy

As an educator with a focus on global education, I believe that education is a matter of life and death, the difference between being awake and asleep in every sense; mentally, emotionally, psychologically, ethically, spiritually, and even physically. Since the time of birth we naturally seek out challenges and experiences that provide meaning to us, making the road to self-awareness a long one that begins in childhood. I see my role as a teacher as an opportunity to guide students towards that goal while understanding that I am a small yet significant piece of their personal puzzle that will contribute to eventually forming their adult identities. 

I believe in student centered experiential learning with a purpose. I believe in a  student-tailored learning experience that is not limited to a strict perimeter of teacher edition recommendations, but that includes as many different perspectives, abilities, and learning styles as the number of students in the class. Creativity and adaptability play an important role in the crafting of my lesson plans, delivery of my instruction, and my relationships with students. Throughout my experiences as a teacher, I have relied heavily on feedback from students, whether formally or informally, in order to plan the next course of action, whether as a whole group or one-on-one. I have also used feedback from both students and colleagues for self-reflection, professional development, and personal growth.

I also believe that standards and both quantifiable and non-quantifiable assessments are valuable elements in an educator’s toolbox. Standards and student growth-objectives provide the basis for the goals that students must feel encouraged and motivated to meet in order to eventually be ready to compete in today’s global economy. And like other educators, my prime satisfaction stems from witnessing the growth in maturity and academic progress exhibited by my students at the end of a school year. I also recognize that not all students begin at the same start line. There are many roadblocks in today’s society that affect the academic performance and emotional well-being of students, especially minorities. For this reason, a solid connection and spirit of collaboration between my students’ families, myself, and the school, is of utmost importance. 

Finally, creating a positive environment within the classroom that is conducive to maximizing learning time for students is a priority that cannot be left out. I believe in slowing down to highlight expectations and practice routines, especially in the beginning of a school year. I believe that by slowing down in the beginning, more momentum can be gained later on when students become more proficient in classroom routines.

It can be said that an educator, such as myself,  works hard to create and further develop the collective class culture and individual positive experiences of each student that comes through her door. Meanwhile, aiming for and remaining mindful of the of the powerful effect of awakening that is slowly taking place.
